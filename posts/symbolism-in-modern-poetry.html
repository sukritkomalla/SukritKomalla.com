Symbolism in Modern Poetry

This paper examines the evolution of metaphorical language and systemic symbolism in modern post-war poetry. Through a close reading of mid-century expressionist texts, we map how traditional metaphors for nature and religion shifted to represent fragmented urban landscapes and mechanical isolation.

The Fragmentation of the Image

Prior to the early 20th century, poetic symbolism relied heavily on shared, objective frameworks (such as Christian allegories or Romantic pastoral tropes). With the advent of high modernism, symbols became highly individualized and fragmented. T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* typifies this shift, using disjointed historical fragments to construct a private, complex symbolic language.

"These fragments I have shored against my ruins."
— T.S. Eliot, *The Waste Land* [1]

Linguistic Structures

We analyze the structural syntax of modern poetry through two primary mechanisms:

  1. Metaphorical Juxtaposition: Placing unrelated words side-by-side to force the reader to synthesize a new, third meaning (e.g., Hart Crane's "the iron log of history").
  2. Syntactic Dislocation: Intentionally breaking grammatical rules (enjambment, line breaks, lack of punctuation) to evoke psychological disorientation. E.E. Cummings utilized this extensively to challenge linear reading patterns.

Modern Poetic Symbols

Consider the recurring imagery of the sea in modern verse. Once a symbol of sublime infinity, in post-war poetry it is frequently depicted as a mute, salt-stung boundary—a divider rather than a connector. In this context, the sea represents the boundary of human language, where communication breaks down and only silence remains.
